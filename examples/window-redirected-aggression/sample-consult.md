# Sample Consult: Window-Triggered Aggression

This is a fictional, shortened example. It shows the expected shape of the
answer; it is not veterinary advice for a real animal.

## Intake Follow-Up

Before a full assessment, the skill should ask:

1. Has the cat had a recent physical exam, pain check, dental check, and urinary
   history review?
2. Did the bite require medical care, and was the cat safely separated after the
   incident?
3. What exact body language appeared before contact: freezing, tail lashing,
   piloerection, growling, dilated pupils, or stalking?
4. Can the window area be blocked or made inaccessible for now?
5. Are there any appetite, drinking, litter box, mobility, grooming, or skin
   changes?

## Bottom Line

The pattern is most consistent with redirected aggression triggered by the
outdoor cat, with pain or medical irritability as important differentials. The
immediate priority is preventing another bite, blocking the trigger, and getting
medical rule-outs because the bite broke skin and the behavior is recurring.

## Plan

- Treat human bite wounds seriously and seek medical care when skin is broken.
- Prevent access to the trigger window for now: cover the lower window, close
  blinds, use removable film, or block the room during outdoor-cat activity.
- Separate cats calmly after incidents; do not pick up, punish, yell, or chase.
- Add indoor enrichment away from the trigger zone: food puzzles, play sessions,
  elevated resting sites, and predictable routines.
- After a quiet period, start controlled desensitization and counterconditioning
  only below threshold. Stop before fixation, stalking, growling, or body
  stiffening.
- Book a veterinary exam to check pain, urinary disease, skin disease, dental
  pain, neurologic change, and medication or supplement issues.
- Escalate to a veterinary behaviorist if episodes intensify, injuries continue,
  recovery time lengthens, or the other cat becomes unsafe.
